1) Quintin 2014: 7 Things You Should Know About Tor
   - Tor still works: It most of the time makes you anonymous, although advanced monitoring or software vulnerabilities can sometimes reduce privacy.
   - Tor is not only used by criminals: Journalists, activists, researchers, and ordinary people use it to protect privacy
   - Does not have military backdoor: Even though early development was funded by the U.S. Navy, Tor is open source
   - Running a Tor relay is generally legal: In the U.S., people have not been prosecuted simply for running a Tor relay.
   - Tor is easy to use: Tools like Tor Browser and Tails OS make anonymous browsing simple and easy.
   - Tor can be slower than normal internet: Because traffic is routed through several encrypted nodes.
   - Tor is not perfect: Users can lose anonymity if they misuse it or if attackers monitor both ends of the connection.
  
2)  Hiding Behind the Keyboard: The Tor Browser €
   - In introduction it explains people might communicate online for many reasons, including social, professional, and sometimes criminal activities. Criminals and terrorists may use covert or anonymous communication to hide their identity.
   - It primarily focuses on digital forensic analysis and investigation methods to uncover anonymous activity.
   - The goal is not to teach crime, but to help investigators detect and analyze these secret communications.
   - Its book tells about its history (The Onion Router) was created in 2002 by the U.S. government but is now open-source. Tor allows anonymous internet communication. It continues to evolve with contributions from privacy and security researchers worldwide.
   - The way it works can be explained, tor hides a user’s identity by routing internet traffic through multiple random relays. Data is encrypted in multiple layers.
   - The three main nodes are:
* Entry node – first relay that receives the encrypted traffic
* Middle node – passes traffic between relays
* Exit node – sends traffic to the final destination
  - Tor makes tracking users very difficult but not impossible. Investigators often rely on user mistakes rather than breaking Tor itself.
  - Some common examples include; Exploiting browser vulnerabilities, Using tracking scripts in emails or documents and Open-source intelligence (OSINT) to identify suspects.
